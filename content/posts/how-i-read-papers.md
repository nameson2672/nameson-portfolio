## The Problem

For years I read papers the wrong way: start at the abstract, grind through every section in order, try to understand every detail before moving on. I'd finish a 12-page paper two hours later having understood maybe 60% of it, with no clear picture of why it mattered.

The three-pass method changed that.

## Pass One: The Map (10 minutes)

Don't read. Scan. Your goal is to understand the paper's shape, not its content.

Read the title and abstract once. Then skip to the conclusion. Then skim the section headers and the first sentence of each section. Look at every figure and table — just the captions, not the surrounding text.

By the end of ten minutes, you should be able to answer: What problem does this paper solve? What's the claimed contribution? Do the results look credible?

If the paper isn't worth your time, you know now — before investing hours.

## Pass Two: The Argument (45 minutes)

Now read properly, but skip the math and implementation details. You're trying to understand the argument: what the authors claim, why they believe it, and what evidence they provide.

Pay attention to the related work section — it tells you where this paper sits in the broader conversation. Mark anything you don't understand but don't stop to resolve it yet. Keep a running note of questions.

By the end, you should be able to summarize the paper in three sentences and explain why the results are (or aren't) convincing.

## Pass Three: The Details (2–4 hours, optional)

Only do this for papers you'll implement, cite, or teach. Re-read with full attention. Reconstruct every proof. Question every design decision. Try to identify the weakest assumption.

This pass is what separates familiarity with a paper from actual understanding.

## One Extra Step

After each paper, write two or three sentences in a personal log: what you took away, what questions it raised, what you'd read next. This forces synthesis and makes the paper retrievable months later when you've forgotten the details.

The goal isn't to read more papers. It's to actually absorb the ones you read.
